# Delivery Management Framework

## Purpose

This framework governs how a 200-person technology professional services and managed services organisation manages the flow of work from the point of sale through to financial close. It is built on the belief that alignment and communication, not process compliance, are what determine whether delivery succeeds.

The framework sits above project methodology. It does not prescribe whether a project runs as Agile, PRINCE2, waterfall, or a hybrid. It creates the organisational conditions for any methodology to work.

---

## Guiding Principles

- **Alignment and communication first.** The root cause of most delivery failures is a gap between what was sold, what was understood, and what was delivered. This framework creates structured checkpoints to close that gap continuously.
- **Leading metrics over lagging metrics.** This framework prioritises metrics that allow conversations before problems become crises: engagement health scores, forecast confidence ratings, scope change velocity, and workforce utilisation trajectories.
- **Probabilistic thinking.** No delivery plan is a commitment. It is a probability distribution. Teams and clients are expected to engage with uncertainty, not be shielded from it.
- **People-first decisions through conversation.** Workforce decisions are not made by formula. This framework creates the data and dialogue to support good decisions. It does not predetermine outcomes.

---

## What This Framework Does Not Do

- It does not prescribe a project management methodology.
- It does not set financial targets independently of the business plan.
- It does not replace judgement. Metrics, templates, and cadences are tools for better conversations, not substitutes for experienced Delivery Owners making informed decisions.

## Framework Stages



## Stage 1: Sales to Delivery Transition

This stage ensures everything learned during the sales process is captured and handed to the delivery team before work begins. It covers Gate 1 criteria, roles, the transition meeting, risk identification, team composition, the Delivery Handover Record template, and the full Stage 1 metric set including timing, capability, alignment, capacity, and deal to delivery vehicle profiling variables.

See: [stage-1-sales-to-delivery-transition.md](stage-1-sales-to-delivery-transition.md)

---

## Stage 2: Project, Engagement, and Consulting Delivery

This stage covers the active delivery period across projects, managed service engagements, and consulting retainers. It includes the fortnightly review cadence, the engagement health scoring model, scope and change management obligations, workforce and utilisation guidance, and Stage 2 metrics.

See: [stage-2-project-engagement-delivery.md](stage-2-project-engagement-delivery.md)

---

## Stage 3: Delivery Closure

This stage covers the structured close of each engagement. It includes Gate 2 criteria, roles, the client closure meeting, internal retrospective, practitioner transition planning, the Closure Acknowledgement template, and Stage 3 metrics.

See: [stage-3-delivery-closure.md](stage-3-delivery-closure.md)

---

## Stage 4: Delivery to Finance Transition

This stage covers the financial close of each engagement. It includes Gate 3 criteria, roles, timesheet reconciliation, revenue recognition review, retention management, CRM and knowledge base update, the Finance Handover Record template, and Stage 4 metrics.

See: [stage-4-delivery-to-finance-transition.md](stage-4-delivery-to-finance-transition.md)

### Framework-Level Governance

| Element | Owner | Review frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Framework design and updates | Delivery Director | Annually or on major organisational change |
| Portfolio metrics dashboard | Delivery Director | Monthly |
| Workforce review | Resource Manager + Practice Lead | Monthly |
